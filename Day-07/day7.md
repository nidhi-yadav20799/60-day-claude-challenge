# My Claude Usage Strategy
*Built for: Data Science Graduate Intern — Daily user — Coding, Learning, Research, Career Prep*

---

## Recommended Primary Model
**🟢 Claude Sonnet 5**

This should be your daily driver for 70–80% of your work.

---

## Why This Model Fits Me

- You work across **many domains daily** (Python, ML, data analysis, research, career docs) — Sonnet 5 is the best all-rounder for exactly this kind of mixed, high-frequency workload.
- You're a **heavy daily user**, so cost/speed efficiency matters — Sonnet is far faster and lighter on usage limits than Opus, while still strong enough for real coding and analytical reasoning.
- Your tasks (debugging notebooks, writing scripts, explaining ML concepts, polishing a resume) rarely need Opus-level depth — they need **consistent, fast, reliable competence**, which is Sonnet's sweet spot.
- You'll save Opus for the few moments per week that truly need heavyweight reasoning.

---

## When to Use Haiku
Use **Claude Haiku 4.5** for quick, low-stakes, high-volume tasks where speed beats depth:
- Quick syntax lookups ("what's the pandas method for X")
- Fast fixes to a single function or error message
- Summarizing a short article/paper abstract
- Renaming variables, formatting code, simple boilerplate
- Rapid back-and-forth brainstorming where you don't need deep analysis

**Rule of thumb:** if you could answer it yourself in 10 seconds with a Google search, use Haiku.

---

## When to Use Sonnet
Use **Claude Sonnet 5** for your default, everyday work:
- Writing and debugging Python/ML code
- Data analysis, EDA, feature engineering, model building
- Explaining data science/ML concepts as you learn
- Drafting and refining your resume, portfolio project write-ups, cover letters
- Structuring GitHub READMEs and technical documentation
- Research summaries and literature review support
- General career prep (interview prep, LinkedIn messaging, project planning)

This is where **~75% of your daily interactions** should live.

---

## When to Use Opus
Reserve **Claude Opus 4.8** for genuinely hard, high-stakes, or multi-step work:
- Designing/architecting a full ML pipeline or complex portfolio project from scratch
- Debugging a gnarly, multi-file bug or a model that's silently underperforming
- Deep research synthesis across many papers/sources with nuanced tradeoffs
- High-stakes career decisions (e.g., comparing job offers, negotiating, crafting a strategy for landing full-time roles)
- Any task where a wrong or shallow answer would cost you real time or a real opportunity

**Rule of thumb:** if getting it wrong is expensive (in time, grade, or career outcome), escalate to Opus.

---

## Recommended Effort Level

### Low
Quick facts, syntax help, simple rewrites, fast iteration on something you'll refine yourself anyway.

### Standard (Default — use most often)
Your default for day-to-day coding, learning support, and writing. Good balance of quality and speed — don't override it unless you have a reason to.

### High
Turn this up for:
- Debugging tricky code
- Concept explanations where you need real depth (not just a definition)
- Resume/portfolio content you'll actually submit
- Research summaries you'll rely on for a report or decision

### Max
Reserve for rare, high-stakes moments:
- Final review pass on a portfolio project before publishing/submitting
- Architecting a complex pipeline or system design
- Deep research task with many sources and real conclusions to draw
- Critical career decisions (offer comparisons, negotiation strategy)

**Practical tip:** effort and model are separate dials. Combine **Sonnet 5 + High effort** as your "serious work" setting, and save **Opus + Max** for the handful of tasks per week that truly need it — this keeps your usage limits from draining on things that didn't need it.

## Biggest Mistakes I Should Avoid

1. **Using Opus for everyday coding.** It's overkill for routine debugging and will burn through your usage limits fast — save it for genuinely hard problems.
2. **Using Max effort by default.** It's slower and eats your rate limits faster with little benefit on tasks that aren't actually hard — reserve it for high-stakes work.
3. **Using Haiku for anything you'll submit or rely on.** It's great for speed, not for depth — don't use it for resume content, research conclusions, or complex code.
4. **Not escalating when it matters.** If you're debugging something for 30+ minutes on Sonnet with no progress, or a decision affects your career, switch to Opus + High/Max rather than grinding away at Standard.
5. **Treating every research question the same.** A quick fact-check ≠ a literature review. Match effort to actual stakes and complexity, not habit.
6. **Forgetting you can switch mid-conversation.** You don't need to start a new chat to escalate — bump the model or effort level right when a task gets harder.

---

## Final Recommendation

**If I could use only ONE model and ONE effort level for most of my work, I'd recommend: Claude Sonnet 5 + Standard effort.**

**Why:** It's the best all-around fit for your daily reality — a mix of coding, learning, research, and career prep where consistency and speed matter more than maximum depth on every single message. Standard effort keeps responses fast and usage-efficient for your high daily volume, while Sonnet 5 is still strong enough to handle real coding problems, explain concepts clearly, and write polished career documents.

Keep Opus + High/Max effort in your back pocket for the small percentage of moments — architecture decisions, deep research, final portfolio polish, career-defining choices — where the extra depth genuinely changes the outcome. That combination (Sonnet 5 as your everyday engine, Opus as your occasional power tool) will give you the best balance of quality, speed, and usage efficiency as a daily user.
